# Headlines Comparison: Label Decks vs. Message Decks

> Fictional example: **Clarion** — a B2B SaaS that replaces manual compliance reporting
> for mid-market financial services firms.

Read just the headlines of each version aloud. One is a table of contents.
The other is an investment thesis.

## GOOD: Narrative message headlines

1. Compliance officers spend 23 hours per report — then pray the auditor doesn't find errors
2. We were those compliance officers. 14 years inside bank operations taught us where reports break
3. One integration replaces a 6-person reporting team's manual work in 90 seconds
4. 127 firms already run live — zero audit findings in 18 months of production
5. $31B spent annually on compliance labor that software hasn't touched yet
6. Banks can't rip out their core systems, so we sit on top and translate
7. Net revenue retention: 138%. Firms start with one regulation, then hand us the rest
8. Every contract includes annual audit-defense SLA — that's why churn is 2%
9. The compliance vendor landscape is fragmented, underfunded, and 10 years behind

13. $3.1M ARR, 22 months. 80% of pipeline comes from auditor referrals we didn't pay for

15. $12M to capture mid-market compliance reporting before the Big Four build their own
16. We turn regulatory burden into a retention moat — every new rule makes switching harder

## Key headline annotations

### Slide 1 — The opening hook

> Compliance officers spend 23 hours per report — then pray the auditor doesn't find errors

The BAD version says "Title Slide" — a wasted first impression. The GOOD version puts the
investor inside the pain immediately. Specific number (23 hours), specific consequence
(audit errors), specific emotion (pray). An investor who has ever dealt with compliance
costs is already nodding.

### Slide 4 — Proof before the market slide

> 127 firms already run live — zero audit findings in 18 months of production

Most decks put traction on slide 9 or 10 — after the investor has already mentally
categorized the company. Placing hard proof early ("127 firms," "zero audit findings")
reframes everything that follows. The market slide lands differently when the investor
already believes the product works.

### Slide 10 — Reframing competition as an advantage

> We don't compete with the Big Four — we make their audits faster, so they recommend us

The BAD version says "Competitive Advantages" — which signals defensiveness. The GOOD
version flips the frame: the scariest potential competitor is actually a distribution
channel. This is the kind of insight that makes a partner say "that's clever" in the
Monday meeting.

### Slide 16 — The closer that echoes the thesis

> We turn regulatory burden into a retention moat — every new rule makes switching harder

The BAD version ends with "The Ask" — a label that gives the investor nothing to
remember. The GOOD version ties the entire narrative together: the same regulatory
complexity that creates the pain (slide 1) becomes the moat (slide 16). The investor
leaves the meeting with a thesis they can repeat: "regulation is the moat."
