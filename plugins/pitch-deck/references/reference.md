# Pitch Deck Reference — Frameworks and Methodology

Detailed reference material for the pitch-deck skill. Inspired by Gleb Maltsev's public speaking
course.

## The Human Problem

Audiences are social animals running on heuristics. They skim, not listen. They're prediction
machines building mental models. Prior experience beats new information ~90% of the time (the honey
bee waggle dance problem).

**Implication**: You must engineer meaning, not dump information.

## Four Pillars of Pitch Prep

### 1. Writing

- Short sentences, metaphors, analogies, vivid examples
- No jargon — real examples only
- Your story in under 60 seconds
- Tech solves problems, it doesn't chase them — validate first

### 2. Delivery

- Tone, volume, inflections, eye contact, gestures, facial expressions
- Vary pace — monotone = audience death
- Practice until it feels natural, not rehearsed
- "Left hook, right hook" — keep alternating between visceral and logical

### 3. Visuals

- Clean slides, photos (not stock), data visualization
- Demos break — always have static backups
- Billboard design: readable at highway speed
- 5-second test on every slide

### 4. Q&A

- Answer hard questions with prepared depth
- Stress test with hostile critics before the real thing
- Reframing technique: pivot hard questions to your strengths
- Each investor is different — do homework, fine-tune responses

## The VC Decision Process

### What VCs Actually Evaluate

```text
who (team) > why (conviction) > what (problem) > how (solution)

Earlier the stage, stronger the ">"
```

### The IC Meeting Reality

Most IC discussions aren't about product, models, or decks. They reduce to: "Is this founder
special?"

**"Special Person: Yes"** — the single most important line in any investment memo.

What makes a founder "special":

- World-class at something that matters for THIS business
- Technical depth others can't touch
- Insight from living the problem for years
- Execution speed that makes normal timelines look slow
- Scar tissue from doing the impossible once already

The partner needs to feel: "I don't know how big this gets, but this person will figure something
out."

### Engineering the Takeaways

You're selling the partnership THROUGH your sponsor VC. Your job:

1. Give the partner 3-5 talking points to champion you
2. Pre-defang every objection they'll face from other partners
3. Make their case easy to make in IC

## The Onion Theory of Risk (Andreessen / Ratcliffe)

A startup on day one = every conceivable kind of risk.

Running a startup = peeling away layers of risk by achieving milestones.

**Risk categories**:

- Founding team risk
- Product risk
- Technical risk
- Launch risk
- Market acceptance risk
- Revenue risk
- Cost of sale risk
- Viral/growth risk

**The pitch framework**:

- "I raised seed, achieved these milestones, eliminated these risks"
- "I raised the A, achieved these milestones, eliminated these risks"
- "Now I'm raising B. Here are my milestones, here are my risks"
- "By the time I raise C, here's the state I'll be in"

**Calibrate money raised to risks being pulled out of the business.**

### Stage-Specific Risk Peeling

| Stage    | What You De-Risk                                     |
| -------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Seed     | Founding team, product, initial launch               |
| Series A | Full product, recruiting, first 5 beta customers     |
| Series B | Unit economics, scalability, repeatable sales motion |

## Seven Risk Categories (De-Risking Checklist)

Pitching = de-risking your company. Rank your top 3 and address them head-on.

### 1. Problem Risk

- How big is the problem? Is it quantifiable?
- Is the problem validated? How?
- How invested are you? Background in the industry?

### 2. Solution / Product Risk

- Have a prototype / MVP?
- What's unique compared to status quo?
- Showcase roadmap (proves you know customer needs)
- What's the moat?

### 3. Business Model & Scaling Risk

- How do you get paid?
- How scalable is it?
- How will the business model scale up?
- Why this business model?

### 4. Finances Risk

- Show projections
- Show what you achieve with X amount of funding
- "I want to be sure you know finances"
- When profitable?

### 5. Market & Timing Risk

- Big enough TAM? (bottom-up, not top-down BS)
- How has the market evolved? Exits? IPOs?
- Why now?
- How fast is the market growing? Trends?
- Demonstrate how much % you can capture in X years

### 6. Team Risk

- Why you and your co-founders?
- Why are YOU the CEO who will make this happen?
- How strong is the founder bond?
- Previous exits? Successes together?
- Equity distribution?

### 7. Exit Risk

- Show commitment to work 5-10 years on this
- How do you want to exit?
- What milestones before exit conversations begin?

## Elevator Pitch Template

Goal: get the other person interested in 30 seconds (3-4 breaths).

No complex tech talk. No marketing BS. Straight to the point.

**Questions in their head**:

- Who are you?
- What do you do?
- How do you do it?
- Who do you do it for?
- Why now?

**Formula**:

"We help [target audience] who struggle with [problem] by offering [solution], which [key benefits].
Unlike [alternative], our solution [unique differentiator]. So far, we've [traction/achievement],
and we're now [future plan/ask]."

## Seven Moats Framework

### 1. Process Power

Last 10% to 99% reliability = 10-100x effort of a hackathon demo. Competitors won't do the
painstaking edge case work.

### 2. Cornered Resource

Regulatory approvals, unique data access, customer relationships, DoD contracts, proprietary
datasets from enterprise customers.

### 3. Switching Costs

- Traditional: data migration pain
- New in AI: 6-12 month pilots building custom workflows
- Consumer: memory/personalization (ChatGPT effect)

### 4. Counter-Positioning

What incumbents can't do without cannibalizing themselves. SaaS charges per-seat; AI startups charge
per-task/outcome.

### 5. Brand

Speed + no legacy business model to protect.

### 6. Network Effects

More users → more data → better models → better product → more users.

### 7. Scale Economies

Massive upfront investment creates cost advantages. Less relevant at application layer.

## Value Proposition Framework

### Hard Value (Objective)

- Cost savings vs. alternatives
- Revenue increase (measurable)
- Time savings (quantifiable)
- Risk reduction (demonstrable)
- Target achievement (trackable)

### Soft Value (Subjective)

- Brand values and trust
- Certainty and reliability
- Status and identity
- Meaning and purpose
- Relationship quality

**Critical**: "People can't think statistically. People are hardwired to think in terms of
narratives." — Neal Stephenson

We feel first, rationalize after. You need BOTH hard and soft value.

## Traction Rules

### Traction vs. Action

| Traction (GOOD)                   | Action (BAD)                  |
| --------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| 500 users in 3 weeks              | Built the product             |
| $10K MRR                          | Talked to 50 customers        |
| 3 signed LOIs                     | Joined an accelerator         |
| 40% week-over-week growth         | Hired a designer              |
| "We couldn't onboard fast enough" | "We're building partnerships" |

### Gold Dust Filter

Identify your top 5 most impressive points. Let those shine.

- The more words, the less impressive
- Stop over-explaining. Start filtering
- If your mom doesn't know what your company does, you have a problem
- A detailed chart won't help in a 3-minute presentation

## Slide Design Specifications

### The 5-Second Test

Show slide for 5 seconds, remove it. Can the viewer state the message?

- One message per slide
- Eyes should know where to focus
- Test multiple versions of same slide

### Text Rules

- Max 4-5 lines per slide
- Max 30 words (40 absolute max)
- All text >20pt (readable) or <13pt (decorative)
- No full sentences, no periods
- Single lines per bullet
- Centered text preferred

### Visual Rules

- Lots of white space
- No edge-to-edge content
- No mixed messages on single slide
- Photos > graphics, real > stock
- High-quality images only
- Streamline: one font family, limited colors, no distracting graphics
- Signal:Clutter ratio is your KPI

### Headline Writing (THE litmus test)

**The Headlines-Tell-The-Story test is the #1 quality gate for any deck.**

- Extract all headlines in order. Read them aloud. Do they make you want to invest?
- If the answer is no, the deck fails — regardless of how good the content is
- Message, not title: "Team engineered for this opportunity" not "Team"
- Should tell complete story when read sequentially without body text
- Punchy, emotional, narrative-driven
- Each headline = one clear takeaway
- Build momentum: open big, alternate visceral/logical, close with conviction
- Test: hand someone just the headline list. Do they get the story?

## Investor Targeting Process

1. Research each investor's last 6-10 investments
2. Identify their thesis, biases, fund status
3. Order: five least important first (practice rounds)
4. Get to target VCs after sufficient practice and pitch fluency
5. Fine-tune commercial and emphasis per investor
6. Add to burning questions list after every meeting

## Anti-Patterns (Things That Kill Pitches)

- Starting with irrelevant top-down market numbers
- Using superlatives without proof
- Hiding competition or risks
- Monotone delivery
- Too much text on slides
- Jargon without real examples
- Cherry-picked validation data
- Numbers that don't match across slides
- Not knowing your own financials cold
- Talking more than listening in Q&A
- Not having appendix slides ready
- Approaching target investors before practice rounds
